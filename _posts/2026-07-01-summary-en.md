## [Supreme Court: Warrant Required for Cell Phone Location Data from Third Parties](https://www.androidpolice.com/supreme-court-protects-your-cell-phone-location-data-after-googles-role-in-a-conviction/) ⭐️ 9.0/10

The US Supreme Court ruled 6-3 on June 29 that law enforcement must obtain a warrant to access cell phone location data held by third-party companies like Google, extending Fourth Amendment protections to such data. This landmark decision significantly strengthens digital privacy rights, requiring police to get a warrant before obtaining location data from tech companies. It directly impacts the legality of geofence warrants and other reverse search warrants used in criminal investigations. The case involved a 2019 bank robbery where police used a geofence warrant to force Google to provide location data. Justice Elena Kagan authored the majority opinion, stating that individuals have a reasonable expectation of privacy in their cell phone location records, even when held by third parties.

**Background**: Geofence warrants are search warrants that allow law enforcement to search a company's database for all mobile devices within a specific area. Previously, the 'third-party doctrine' allowed access to data voluntarily shared with companies. This ruling rejects that doctrine for sensitive location data, expanding Fourth Amendment protections in the digital age.

Google Research has open-sourced TimesFM 2.5, an updated pretrained foundation model for time-series forecasting with 200M parameters, up to 16k context length, and support for continuous quantile forecasting. Foundation models like TimesFM are pretrained on massive time-series data and can generalize to new tasks without retraining, similar to large language models. TimesFM uses a decoder-only transformer architecture, which employs causally masked self-attention and feedforward networks to generate forecasts autoregressively.
